Functions of the Urinary System
Filtration
Kidneys filter ~180 L of blood plasma per day; remove waste products
Excretion
Eliminate metabolic waste (urea, creatinine, uric acid) in urine
Fluid balance
Regulate water and electrolyte balance in the body
Acid-base balance
Maintain blood pH by excreting H⁺ and reabsorbing bicarbonate
Blood pressure
Regulate BP via renin-angiotensin system and fluid volume
Hormone production
Produce erythropoietin (RBC production) and activate Vitamin D
Specialty: Urology · Specialist: Urologist  |  Nephrology · Nephrologist (kidney medicine)  |  Key cf: nephr/o (kidney), ren/o (kidney), cyst/o (bladder), urin/o (urine)
Urinary Tract — At a Glance
Kidneys (nephr/o, ren/o) Renal pelvis (pyel/o) Ureters (ureter/o) Bladder (cyst/o, vesic/o) Trigone (trigon/o) Urethra (urethr/o) Urinary meatus (meat/o)
Micturition = urination = voiding — all mean the act of passing urine
Key Urinary Terms
Micturition / Voiding / Urination — act of passing urine
Urine (urin/o, ur/o) — waste fluid produced by kidneys
Azotemia (azot/o) — excess nitrogenous waste in blood
Diuresis — increased urine production
Urgency — sudden strong urge to urinate
Hilum (hil/o) — medial indentation where vessels/ureter enter kidney
Urinary Tract — Anatomy & Flow
1
Kidneysnephr/o, ren/o
Bean-shaped organs in retroperitoneal space (behind peritoneum); filter blood → produce urine. Hilum = medial notch where renal artery, vein, and ureter enter/exit.
2
Renal Pelvispyel/o
Funnel-shaped basin in the kidney that collects urine from the calyces and channels it into the ureter. Pyelonephritis = infection of renal pelvis + kidney.
3
Uretersureter/o
Two muscular tubes (~25–30 cm) that carry urine from renal pelvis to bladder via peristalsis. Ureteral obstruction by a stone causes severe renal colic.
4
Urinary Bladdercyst/o, vesic/o
Hollow muscular reservoir that stores urine (capacity ~400–600 mL). Trigone (trigon/o) = smooth triangular area at base formed by two ureteral openings and the urethral opening.
5
Urethraurethr/o
Tube from bladder to outside. Female: ~4 cm. Male: ~20 cm (passes through prostate). Controlled by internal and external sphincters.
6
Urinary Meatusmeat/o
The external opening of the urethra — the exit point for urine from the body.
Kidney Internal Anatomy
Cortex — outer region; contains glomeruli and proximal tubules
Medulla — inner region; contains loops of Henle and collecting ducts
Calyx (pl. calyces) — cup-shaped structures collecting urine from pyramids
Renal pelvis (pyel/o) — collects urine → ureter
Hilum (hil/o) — medial notch; entry/exit of vessels and ureter
Urethra: Male vs. Female
Female urethra
~4 cm; short → more susceptible to UTIs. Urethra, vagina, and anus are separate openings.
Male urethra
~20 cm; longer — passes through prostate gland. Shared pathway for urine and semen. Prostate enlargement (BPH) can obstruct flow.
The Nephron — Functional Unit of the Kidney
Each kidney contains ~1 million nephrons. The nephron produces urine through 3 processes: Filtration → Reabsorption → Secretion
1
Glomerulus
Filtration: knot of capillaries inside Bowman's capsule. Blood is filtered under pressure — water, glucose, ions, and small molecules pass into the capsule (filtrate). Blood cells and proteins are retained.
2
Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT)
Reabsorption: ~65% of filtrate reabsorbed here — glucose, amino acids, sodium, water, bicarbonate.
3
Loop of Henle
Descending limb: water reabsorption. Ascending limb: sodium and chloride reabsorbed. Creates osmotic gradient for concentration of urine.
4
Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT)
Secretion & fine-tuning: regulates potassium, acid-base balance; ADH and aldosterone act here.
5
Collecting Duct
Final concentration of urine; ADH controls water reabsorption. Urine then passes to calyx → renal pelvis → ureter.
3 Nephron Processes
1. Filtration
Glomerulus → Bowman's capsule. Pressure forces small molecules out of blood into filtrate.
2. Reabsorption
Tubules reclaim useful substances (glucose, water, amino acids) back into blood.
3. Secretion
Tubules actively move additional waste products (H⁺, drugs) FROM blood INTO filtrate.
Normal Urine Characteristics
Volume — 1,000–2,000 mL/day (average ~1,500 mL)
Color — pale yellow to amber (from urochrome)
pH — 4.5–8.0 (average ~6.0, slightly acidic)
Specific gravity — 1.005–1.030
Normal: no glucose, no protein, no blood, no bacteria
Urinalysis (UA) is the primary lab test for urinary conditions — tests color, pH, specific gravity, glucose, protein, blood, leukocytes, and nitrites
Pyelonephritis
Bacterial infection of the renal pelvis AND kidney. pyel/o + nephr/o + -itis. Most common from ascending UTI (E. coli). Symptoms: flank pain, fever, CVA tenderness, dysuria.
Glomerulonephritis
Inflammation of the glomeruli; often immune-mediated. Causes: strep infection, autoimmune (IgA nephropathy, lupus). Signs: hematuria, proteinuria, hypertension, edema.
Nephrotic syndrome
Glomerular damage → massive proteinuria (>3.5 g/day), hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia
Nephroptosis
Drooping or downward displacement of a kidney. nephr/o + -ptosis (drooping). Also called floating kidney.
Polycystic kidney disease (PKD)
Hereditary; multiple cysts replace kidney tissue; progressive renal failure; flank pain, hematuria, hypertension
Acute kidney injury (AKI)
Sudden loss of kidney function; causes: dehydration (prerenal), obstruction (postrenal), or direct damage (intrarenal); elevated BUN and creatinine
Chronic kidney disease (CKD)
Progressive loss of kidney function over months/years; 5 stages based on GFR; end stage requires dialysis or transplant. azot/o + -emia = azotemia (nitrogenous waste buildup)
Cystitis
Bladder infection/inflammation. cyst/o + -itis. Most common UTI; more common in females; caused by E. coli. Symptoms: dysuria, frequency, urgency, hematuria.
Cystocele
Herniation of the bladder into the vagina. cyst/o + -cele (hernia/bulge). Caused by weakened pelvic floor; common after childbirth. Symptoms: pressure, incontinence.
Urethral stenosis
Narrowing of the urethra. urethr/o + stenosis. Obstructs urinary flow; causes weak stream, retention, recurrent UTIs. Treatment: meatotomy or dilation.
Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR)
Backflow of urine from bladder into ureters/kidneys. vesic/o + ureter/o + re- + -flux. Risk for ascending pyelonephritis; common in children.
Urinary incontinence
Involuntary leakage of urine. Types: stress (cough/sneeze), urge (overactive bladder), overflow, mixed. in- + continent
Urinary retention
Inability to empty the bladder fully; acute or chronic. Causes: BPH, urethral stenosis, neurogenic bladder. Treatment: catheterization.
Bladder carcinoma
Most common urinary malignancy; transitional cell carcinoma; painless hematuria is classic presentation; risk: smoking, chemical exposure
Anuria
Absence of urine output (<50 mL/day). an- (without) + -uria (urine condition). Medical emergency; indicates severe renal failure.
Oliguria
Decreased urine output (50–400 mL/day). olig/o + -uria. Sign of dehydration or renal failure.
Polyuria
Excessive urine production (>3 L/day). poly- (many) + -uria. Associated with diabetes mellitus, diabetes insipidus, diuretics.
Dysuria
Painful or difficult urination. dys- (difficult) + -uria. Classic symptom of UTI, urethritis, kidney stones.
Hematuria
Blood in urine. hemat/o + -uria. Gross (visible) or microscopic. Causes: UTI, stones, tumor, trauma.
Proteinuria
Protein in urine. protein + -uria. Indicates glomerular damage. Massive proteinuria = nephrotic syndrome.
Glycosuria
Glucose in urine. glycos/o + -uria. Abnormal; occurs when blood glucose exceeds renal threshold (~180 mg/dL). Classic sign of uncontrolled diabetes mellitus.
Nocturia
Excessive urination at night; disrupts sleep; associated with BPH, diabetes, heart failure
Pyuria
Pus in urine. py/o + -uria. Indicates infection (UTI, pyelonephritis, STI). WBCs visible on microscopy.
Azoturia / Azotemia
Excess nitrogenous waste (urea, creatinine) in urine/blood. azot/o = nitrogen. Indicates renal insufficiency.
-uria suffix pattern: a- (no) · olig/o (little) · poly- (much) · dys- (painful) · hemat/o (blood) · glycos/o (glucose) · py/o (pus) · noct/i (night) · protein (protein)
Important distinction: Diabetes insipidus and Diabetes mellitus are very different diseases that share the prefix di- (through/across) and symptom of polyuria and polydipsia.
Diabetes Insipidus (DI)
Cause: Deficiency of ADH (antidiuretic hormone) OR kidney resistance to ADH
Mechanism: Collecting ducts cannot reabsorb water → massive dilute urine output
Symptoms: Polyuria (may exceed 20 L/day!), polydipsia (intense thirst)
Urine: Very dilute (low specific gravity, no glucose)
Blood glucose: NORMAL
Treatment: Desmopressin (synthetic ADH)
Diabetes Mellitus (DM)
Cause: Insulin deficiency (Type 1) or insulin resistance (Type 2)
Mechanism: Elevated blood glucose exceeds renal threshold → osmotic diuresis → glucose pulls water into urine
Symptoms: Polyuria, polydipsia (poly- = many), polyphagia (increased appetite)
Urine: Contains glucose (glycosuria) and possibly ketones
Blood glucose: ELEVATED
Treatment: Insulin (Type 1), metformin/lifestyle (Type 2)
Urolithiasis / Nephrolithiasis
Urinary tract stones. lith/o + -iasis. Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis). Most common type: calcium oxalate stones. Severe colicky flank pain radiating to groin.
Renal colic
Severe intermittent pain caused by stone passing through ureter; one of the most painful conditions known; waves of pain, nausea, vomiting, hematuria
Stone types
Calcium oxalate (most common, ~80%) · Struvite (infection-related) · Uric acid (gout, dehydration) · Cystine (hereditary)
Nephrolithotomy
Surgical incision into kidney to remove a stone. nephr/o + lith/o + -tomy (incision). For large stones not amenable to ESWL.
Lithotripsy / ESWL
Crushing of stones. lith/o + -tripsy (crushing). ESWL = extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy; sound waves break stone into passable fragments.
Stone treatment by size: Small (<4 mm) → hydration + wait. Medium (4–10 mm) → ESWL or ureteroscopy. Large (>10 mm) → percutaneous nephrolithotomy.
Diagnostic Procedures
Urinalysis (UA)
urin/o + -analysis; complete physical, chemical, microscopic exam of urine; most important urinary test; detects infection, diabetes, kidney disease
Urine culture & sensitivity (C&S)
Identifies bacterial organisms and antibiotic sensitivities; gold standard for UTI diagnosis
Cystoscopy (cyst/o + -scopy)
Direct visual examination of the bladder interior via a cystoscope (cyst/o + -scope); diagnoses tumors, stones, bleeding; can perform biopsies and remove small stones
Voiding cystourethrography (VCUG)
vesic/o + urethr/o + -graphy; X-ray imaging during urination; diagnoses vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) and urethral stenosis
Intravenous pyelogram (IVP)
pyel/o + -gram; IV contrast dye excreted by kidneys; X-ray shows renal pelvis, ureters, bladder; evaluates stones, tumors, obstruction
Renal ultrasound
Imaging of kidneys and bladder; first-line for hydronephrosis, stones, cysts, tumors; no radiation
BUN & creatinine
Blood tests measuring nitrogenous waste; elevated in renal failure; creatinine more specific for GFR
GFR (glomerular filtration rate)
Measures kidney function; calculated from serum creatinine, age, sex; normal >90 mL/min; staging CKD
Therapeutic Procedures
Urinary drainage
Catheterization
Insertion of a catheter (flexible tube) into bladder via urethra to drain urine; for retention, surgery, incontinence management; Foley catheter = indwelling
Nephrostomy
nephr/o + -stomy; percutaneous tube into renal pelvis to drain urine; used when ureter is obstructed
Kidney procedures
Nephropexy (nephr/o + -pexy)
Surgical fixation of a floating/drooping kidney (-pexy = fixation). Treats nephroptosis.
Nephrolithotomy
nephr/o + lith/o + -tomy; kidney incision to remove stone
Lithotripsy (ESWL)
lith/o + -tripsy; crushing of urinary stones via shock waves
Nephrectomy
nephr/o + -ectomy; surgical removal of kidney; for cancer, donation, severe infection
Urethral & bladder
Meatotomy (meat/o + -tomy)
Incision of the urinary meatus (external urethral opening) to widen it; treats meatal stenosis
Cystectomy
cyst/o + -ectomy; surgical removal of the bladder; for bladder cancer; requires urinary diversion
Renal replacement
Hemodialysis Peritoneal dialysis Renal transplant
Pharmacology
Diuretics Antibiotics (UTI) Analgesics (renal colic) Desmopressin (DI) ACE inhibitors (CKD)
